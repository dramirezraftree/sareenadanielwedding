The Weekend

Wedding Events

Five celebrations across two luminous days in Goa — each with its own palette, spirit, and story.

Mehendi
Friday Afternoon · November 27

🎨 Temple Garden Pastels

Mehendi is how the weekend begins — an afternoon gathering where intricate henna designs are applied to the bride's hands and feet. Guests who'd like henna applied are welcome to join in too. In Bengali tradition, the patterns often hide the groom's initials somewhere in the design of the bride's henna, and the darkness of the henna is said to reflect the depth of his love. It's relaxed and festive, with music playing and everyone settling into the weekend together. A good afternoon to arrive unhurried.

Sangeet means "music together," and that's more or less what it is. In most Indian weddings, the Sangeet is an evening of performances — songs, dances, and routines put together by family and friends from both sides. Traditionally an intimate gathering of women singing folk compositions, it has evolved into something closer to a celebration in its own right. Expect performances, toasts, and a lot of dancing.

The Haldi — or gaye holud, meaning "yellow on the body" — is one of the most intimate rituals of a Bengali wedding. A paste of turmeric, sandalwood, and rose water is applied to the bride and groom by family, a blessing rooted in the belief that turmeric purifies and protects. It's warm, unhurried, and genuinely a little messy. Wear something you're comfortable in — within the citrus palette — and don't expect to leave clean.

Wedding Ceremony

The Bengali wedding ceremony — Biye — is built from rituals that have been practiced for centuries, each carrying its own significance. The bride's entrance begins with the shubho drishti, a sacred first look between the couple. The ceremony moves through the saptapadi, seven vows taken together, and culminates in the sindur daan — the moment the groom places vermilion in the part of the bride's hair. A pandit guides the ceremony through Sanskrit mantras, with fire, flowers, and conch shells woven throughout. It is meaningful, unhurried, and unlike anything else.

The Reception follows the ceremony and is exactly what it sounds like — a chance to celebrate, eat well, and spend the evening with the people who made the trip. In Bengali tradition, the bou bhat is a feast that formally welcomes the bride into her new family; our reception carries that spirit.
